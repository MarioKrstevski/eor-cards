• Fetal position

    • If the baby’s head is too large for the maternal pelvis → Cephalopelvic Disproportion (CPD).

      • This can cause labor dystocia = difficult or obstructed labor.

      • May need C-section

    • Macrosomia

      • Birth weight ≥ 4500 g (≈10 lbs) or ≥ 90th percentile for gestational age

      • Causes:

§  Maternal diabetes (gestational or preexisting)

§  Obesity

§  Post-term pregnancy (> 42 weeks)

§  Previous large infant

  • Complications

    • Shoulder dystocia – shoulders get stuck behind

    • This can cause clavicle fracture, hypoxia, brachial plexus injury

  • Fetal attitude

    • Fetal attitude refers to how the baby’s body parts are positioned in relation to each other — essentially how curled up or stretched out the baby is inside the womb. The normal, or ideal, fetal attitude is full flexion, where the baby is tucked into a compact, rounded shape with the chin resting on the chest, arms and legs pulled in, and the back curved. This position allows the smallest head diameter (about 9.5 cm) to pass through the birth canal, making delivery easier. In contrast, abnormal attitudes occur when the baby’s head is not fully flexed. In partial extension (brow presentation), the forehead leads the way, creating a larger head diameter and making delivery more difficult. In hyperextension (face presentation), the head is tilted so far back that the face comes first, which presents the largest head diameter and often requires a cesarean delivery. In simple terms, a flexed baby is well-positioned for birth, while an extended baby can make delivery harder.
